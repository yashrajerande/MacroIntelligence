SIGNAL 01 · CREDIT CYCLE
✦ Positive
Credit-Deposit Mismatch Signals Liquidity Strain
Bank credit growth at 14.3% YoY vastly outpaces deposit growth of 10.6% YoY. CD ratio surged to 83.04% (10y: 100th percentile), up from 82.38% prior period. Deposit mobilization lagging credit expansion by 370 bps.
The banking system is approaching a structural liquidity ceiling. At 83.04% CD ratio (highest decile in 10 years), banks face mounting pressure to either slow credit or raise deposits at higher cost. This constrains the RBI's ability to sustain the easing cycle and threatens credit quality if weaker borrowers are funded at margin. Capital allocators should monitor bank NPA trajectories and deposit competition intensity—this is the binding constraint on growth, not policy rate.

SIGNAL 07 · UNDER THE RADAR
⚡ Surprise
Airline Passenger Collapse Signals Demand Cliff Ahead
Airline passenger traffic at 35.08M (2nd percentile), down sharply from 38.59M prior. This is the lowest reading in the dataset's 10-year history. Concurrent data: GST collections at ₹200,064 crore (91st percentile), PV sales +10.5% YoY, 2W sales +35.2% YoY (91st percentile), CV sales +28.89% YoY (84th percentile).
This is the critical divergence: high-frequency consumption indicators (GST, vehicle sales) are screaming growth, yet airline traffic—the most discretionary, income-elastic consumption metric—has collapsed to a 10-year low. This is not a seasonal dip; it's a structural break. The most likely explanation: (1) price elasticity has kicked in—airfares have risen sharply, pricing out middle-income travelers, or (2) a shift in travel patterns (rail, road) is capturing demand, or (3) most concerning—high-income consumer confidence has cracked despite headline growth data. The fact that GST and vehicle sales remain strong suggests the collapse is NOT broad-based demand destruction, but rather a bifurcation: mass-market consumption (vehicles, FMCG via GST) is holding, but discretionary high-income spending (air travel) is contracting. This is a leading indicator of margin compression in premium segments and a warning that the 'broad-based consumption expansion' narrative is masking underlying income inequality stress. For allocators: avoid premium discretionary plays (airlines, luxury retail, high-end hospitality); rotate toward mass-market consumption and value segments. This airline signal is the canary in the coal mine for the next 2-3 quarters.
