Executive Summary
▾
01
India Macro Regime

7.8% GDP and a 56.5 PMI Composite are the headline; the subtext is a regime fracturing along three fault lines simultaneously. PMI manufacturing fell 300bps to 53.9 in a single month — core sector decelerated from 4.7% to 2.3% YoY — while airline passengers dropped 9% to a decade-low 35 million, even as GST printed a record ₹2.01 lakh crore. India's dual economy is not converging; it is diverging — the formal economy's tax receipts are rising while the affluent consumer's discretionary spend is contracting. ₹2.01 lakh crore in GST is a formalization story as much as a demand story; the airline data is the demand story, and it is pointing the other direction.

02
Global Macro Regime

0.7% US GDP SAAR — down from 4.4% — is the sharpest single-quarter deceleration in the current cycle, yet the S&P 500 at 7,138 (100th percentile) and US VIX at 17.83 (12th percentile) are priced for a soft landing that the growth data does not support. US core PCE ticked up to 3.1% from 3.0% — the Fed's preferred measure is re-accelerating while growth is stalling, a stagflationary combination that eliminates the rate-cut optionality markets are pricing. China GDP at 4.5% (down from 5.0%) with iron ore up 51% to $161.91/tonne suggests stimulus is flowing into commodity markets, not consumption — a pattern that inflates India's import bill without generating export demand for Indian goods.

03
Liquidity Conditions

FII equity net flows reversed from +₹22,615 crore to -₹114 crore — a ₹22,729 crore swing in a single period — while DII absorption at ₹128 crore is negligible, leaving the market without a natural buyer at the margin. SIP inflows at ₹29,845 crore (85th percentile) are structurally supportive, but SIP account growth at 1.6% to 10.45 crore (2nd percentile) reveals the critical distinction: average ticket size is rising because affluent investors are adding, not because new retail participants are entering — the SIP base is not broadening. Corporate bond issuance at ₹1.12 lakh crore (7th percentile) signals that banks are rationing credit and forcing corporates to capital markets at a moment when the CD ratio at 83.04% leaves little room for deposit-funded lending expansion. US 10Y at 4.35% (78th percentile) is widening the India-US yield differential at precisely the moment FIIs are exiting — the carry arithmetic is deteriorating.

04
Equity + Real Estate Implications

Bank Nifty at 55,400 (91st percentile) is at a decade-high valuation while the CD ratio at 83.04% and NBFC credit deceleration from 17% to 13% are signalling funding stress — this is the divergence that historically precedes a risk-weight circular or macro-prudential tightening. Real estate is bifurcating: home loan disbursements at ₹5.5 lakh crore (76th percentile) and average loan rates down to 8.0% from 9.1% are driving residential launches to 1.26 lakh units, but sales at 1.02 lakh units are falling while unsold inventory has jumped to 6 lakh units — launches are outpacing absorption by a widening margin. Office absorption at 29.9 million sqft (24th percentile) with vacancy at 13.9% confirms that commercial real estate is not participating in the residential boom. Retail mall vacancy at 5.7% (11th percentile) is the one commercial bright spot — but e-commerce GMV growth collapsing to 12.4% from 25% suggests the physical retail recovery may be a substitution effect, not incremental demand.

05
Key Risks to Monitor

Three risks, ranked by probability × impact: first, Brent at $103.9 — every $10 adds approximately ₹40,000-50,000 crore to India's annual oil import bill — and WTI at $99.38 (82nd percentile, up from $96.37) signals US supply tightness that could push Brent toward $110-115, flipping fuel inflation positive and adding 130bps to headline CPI within two quarters. Second, the CD ratio at 83.04% with deposit growth at 10.8% and credit at 13.8% — if this divergence persists another quarter, the RBI's macro-prudential toolkit (risk weights, LCR tightening) becomes the most likely policy response, not a rate cut. Third, the affluent consumer retrenchment signal — airline passengers at a decade-low 35 million and e-commerce GMV at 12.4% (15th percentile) — if this spreads to vehicle sales and GST, the consumption pillar of the 7.8% GDP story loses its load-bearing capacity. Watch Friday's RBI liquidity data and the next fortnightly credit-deposit print — if the CD ratio crosses 84%, the policy calculus changes faster than the rate curve is pricing.

Credit Cycle

The Munger inversion: the last time India's CD ratio sustained above 80% during a rate-cut cycle was Q2 FY14; within 12 months, the taper tantrum forced RBI to reverse with emergency hikes. Today's setup rhymes — except NBFC credit growth has already decelerated from 17% to 13%, and corporate bond issuance at ₹1.12 lakh crore sits at the 7th percentile, meaning the credit rationing has begun. Banks are not running out of capital; they are running out of cheap funding — and the regulator has noticed.
