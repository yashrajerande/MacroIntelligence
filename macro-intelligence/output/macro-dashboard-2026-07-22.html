Executive Summary

01
India Macro Regime

7.8% GDP with PMI Composite at 56.5 — down from 58.9 — and core sector at 2.3% YoY (from 4.7%) describes an economy that is expanding at the headline while decelerating in its physical sinews. IIP capital goods at 12.5% is a single-month reversal of a weak 4.3% prior print, not a trend; cement dispatch and steel output, the honest proxies for real investment, are not corroborating it. Consumption is bifurcating sharply: vehicle sales across all segments up 26-29% YoY while airline traffic falls to a 7th-percentile low and e-commerce GMV growth halves to 12.4% — mass durables are booming, affluent discretionary is contracting. GST at ₹2.01 lakh crore is a record, but formalisation of existing informal spend explains a meaningful share of the beat.

02
Global Macro Regime

0.7% US GDP SAAR — down from 4.4% — with US PMI Composite at 50.3 (from 51.9) is the sharpest single-quarter deceleration in the dataset, yet S&P 500 sits at a 100th-percentile all-time high and US VIX at 17.05 signals near-total complacency. US 10Y Treasury at 4.6% — the 83rd percentile — is sticky despite growth weakness, which means the Fed is trapped: cutting risks reigniting inflation (core PCE at 3.1%, up from 3.0%), holding risks accelerating the slowdown. China GDP at 4.5% (from 5.0%) and Eurozone GDP at 0.9% (from 1.2%) confirm the deceleration is not a US idiosyncrasy — global PMI Composite at 51.0 is barely above contraction. Nikkei at a 100th-percentile high despite two sessions of sharp selling is the clearest sign that equity markets globally are priced for a world that the macro data has already left.

03
Liquidity Conditions

FII equity net flows swung from +₹22,615 crore to -₹114 crore in a single period — a reversal of ₹22,729 crore — while DII absorption at +₹128 crore is at the 22nd percentile, far too thin to absorb sustained foreign selling. SIP inflows at ₹29,845 crore remain structurally strong (85th percentile), but SIP account growth at 1.6% MoM — a 2nd-percentile reading — means the inflow engine is running on existing participants deploying more, not new entrants broadening the base. Domestic liquidity is therefore concentrated and fragile: if the cohort of high-AUM SIP investors reduces allocation, there is no second layer of retail demand to absorb the gap. CD ratio at 83.04% means bank balance sheets have limited capacity to expand credit further without either deposit rate wars or macro-prudential intervention from the RBI.

04
Equity + Real Estate Implications

Bank Nifty at the 96th percentile of its 10-year range — even as it edges down from 57,945 to 57,835 — is pricing a credit cycle that the CD ratio at 83.04% and NBFC deceleration to 13% growth are beginning to contradict; NIM compression risk is not in consensus estimates. Real estate is sending a split signal: office absorption at 29.9 msf (24th percentile) and vacancy falling to 13.9% from 17.2% support commercial REITs, yet Embassy, Mindspace, and Brookfield REITs are all trading well below prior-period levels — the market is discounting something the operating metrics are not yet showing. Residential sales at 1.02 lakh units are down from 1.09 lakh while launches at 1.26 lakh units are rising — unsold inventory at 6 lakh units is building, and with home loan rates at 8% already reflecting prior RBI easing, the affordability tailwind is largely spent. India VIX at 12.6 (7th percentile) suggests equity markets are not pricing any of these second-order risks.

05
Key Risks to Monitor

Ranked by probability × impact: first, Brent at $91.56 — every $10 sustained above $90 adds approximately $15 billion to India's import bill, widens CAD by ~40bps, and feeds into CPI with a two-quarter lag at a moment when food inflation is already accelerating to 3.47%. Second, the FII reversal from +₹22,615 crore to -₹114 crore in one period — if DXY at 101.2 continues its four-of-five-period uptrend and US 10Y holds at 4.6%, the carry calculus for EM allocators deteriorates and INR at 96.15 (91st percentile weakness) has no obvious floor. Third, CD ratio at 83.04% with NBFC credit already decelerating — the risk is not a sudden crisis but a slow credit rationing that shows up in SME loan rejection rates and informal sector employment before it appears in any headline number. Watch Thursday's RBI weekly statistical supplement for deposit growth — if it prints below 10.5%, the funding gap widens further and macro-prudential action becomes the base case, not the tail.

SIGNAL 01 · CREDIT CYCLE
⚠ Risk
Deposit Gap Forces RBI's Hand
Bank credit growth at 13.8% YoY (Mar 2026) vs deposit growth at 10.8% — a 300bps divergence widening for consecutive months. CD ratio now 83.04% (81st percentile, 10-year high territory), up from 82.5% last period. NBFC credit growth has decelerated sharply to 13% from 17% YoY, signalling funding stress. Corporate bond issuance spiked to ₹112,000 cr (7th percentile, extreme low — firms are desperate for non-bank funding).
This is a funding crisis in slow motion. Banks are rationing deposits to corporates; NBFCs are being starved. RBI will face pressure to either cut rates (to incentivise deposits) or tighten liquidity (to cool credit demand). Overweight private banks with CASA ratios >45% — they'll poach deposits from weaker peers. Avoid NBFCs with wholesale funding >30% of liabilities; their cost of capital is about to spike. Expect credit growth to decelerate sharply in Q1 FY27.

SIGNAL 07 · UNDER THE RADAR
⚡ Surprise
Airline Collapse Signals Demand Cliff, Not Cycle
Airline passenger traffic at 35.08 (7th percentile, near-decade low), down sharply from 38.59 YoY. This is NOT a seasonal dip — it's a 9% YoY decline in absolute passenger volumes. Simultaneously, e-commerce GMV growth has collapsed to 12.4% from 25% YoY (15th percentile). Yet GST collections remain strong at ₹2.01 lakh cr monthly, and vehicle sales (PV +26.1%, 2W +28.7%, CV +28.9%) are surging. The contradiction: discretionary consumption (air travel, online shopping) is crashing, but durable goods and mass consumption are booming. This is a bifurcation between affluent and mass-market demand.
The affluent consumer is pulling back sharply (airlines, e-commerce are affluent proxies), while mass-market consumption (vehicles, GST-taxed goods) remains resilient. This is a late-cycle warning: the high-income cohort that drove growth in FY24-25 is now cautious. Expect: (1) e-commerce growth to decelerate further as GMV base normalises; (2) airline stocks to underperform despite strong domestic growth narrative; (3) mass-market FMCG and auto to outperform premium segments. Overweight mass-market auto (Maruti, Bajaj, Hero) and value FMCG; underweight premium auto (luxury SUVs) and e-commerce platforms. This bifurcation will persist for 2-3 quarters as affluent households adjust to higher rates and INR weakness.
